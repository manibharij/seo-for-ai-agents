# Ranking factors: the owned-media side

Read this for the detail behind the Rank rung. Ranking is decided by a mix of factors; this file is about the ones **on your own site** that you can actually build, plus an honest map of the ones you can't. The job of this rung is to take the controllable factors as far as they go.

> The split that keeps this honest. **Owned-media (build-time, this rung):** relevance, search-intent match, content quality and depth, on-page E-E-A-T signals, topical authority within your own site. **Off-site / live (not this rung):** backlinks and brand authority (earned media), the strength of the competition, and where you *actually* rank over time (live data). Maximise the first; be honest about the second.

---

## Infer the target intent (don't ask)

Before judging whether a page ranks, you need to know what it's *for*. Work it out yourself:
- Read the page's content, `<h1>`, headings, URL, and its place in the site.
- Infer the **query and intent** a visitor would have when they land here, and the **intent type**: informational ("how/what/why"), commercial ("best/compare/review"), transactional ("buy/price/near me"), or navigational (brand).
- State the inferred intent in your report ("this reads as an informational page targeting 'how X works'"), so the user can correct it if needed, but don't make them supply it.
- If a keyword tool is connected, confirm the intent against the live SERP; if not, infer and label it as inference.

A page can be flawless on rungs 1 to 4 and still not rank because it answers the wrong question. Intent match is the first thing to get right.

---

## The owned-media ranking factors

### Search-intent match
The content must satisfy the intent you inferred, in the visitor's language. Reframe or restructure pages that target one intent but deliver another (the classic "blog post ranking for a buying query" problem). Lead with the answer the searcher wants.

### Genuine quality and depth
Engines rank the page that best, most comprehensively serves the query. Assess honestly against what already ranks:
- Is it specific, accurate, and complete, or thin and generic?
- Does it cover the obvious follow-up questions?
- Does it add something the current top results don't (real data, real experience, a clearer explanation)?
Improve real content (via `seo-content-editing`); where substance is genuinely missing, brief a human, never fabricate it.

### E-E-A-T and trust
Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trust, the signals engines weigh before ranking, and weigh heavily for YMYL (health, finance, legal). On-page, that means: real named authors with genuine credentials, real first-hand detail, cited sources, transparency (about/contact), accuracy. Surface the genuine signals; mirror them in schema (Understand). **Never invent an author, credential, review, or citation** to look more trustworthy, it's a liability and engines increasingly discount manufactured authority.

### Topical authority
Engines favour sites that demonstrably cover a topic deeply, not sites that touch it once. Build the **hub-and-spoke clusters** (pillar page plus supporting pages, interlinked, from Connect and `seo-positioning-strategy`) so the site is a credible source on the topic. A lone page on an island ranks worse than the same page inside a strong cluster.

### Relevance signals
Title, `<h1>`, headings and body should clearly and naturally align with the intent and the language people use, without keyword-stuffing (which is stale and counter-productive). Clarity over density.

## Using live data (optional, never required)
- **Search Console:** pages with impressions but low clicks or position signal an intent/title/quality gap to close; "almost ranking" queries (positions ~5 to 20) are the fastest wins; declining pages need a refresh. Weight your effort by real opportunity.
- **Keyword tool (DataForSEO / Ahrefs):** real demand (is it worth targeting?) and competitive strength (can you realistically win?). Grounds the quality bar in what it actually takes to rank.
- **Without either:** assess on the content and the domain, and **say** the assessment is based on on-page signals, not live data.

---

## The honest boundary (put this in the report)
You can make a page maximally **deserving** of ranking: right intent, genuinely excellent, trustworthy, inside an authoritative cluster. You cannot, from the build, supply the **off-site authority** (links, brand, reputation, earned over time) or out-muscle stronger **competition**, and you cannot promise a position. Those are earned-media and live-data work, a separate, ongoing discipline. So the honest claim is always: "I've done the owned-media work that earns ranking; whether you rank, and where, also depends on factors outside the build, which is the next discipline." Never promise a ranking.
