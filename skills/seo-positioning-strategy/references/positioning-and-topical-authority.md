# Positioning & topical authority — building the plan

Read this to turn "what should we own and how" into a concrete, honest plan. Two pillars: a **positioning** grounded in real differentiators, and a **topical-authority map** that says which topics to own and how to structure the content to own them.

---

## Positioning (real, or it's worthless)

A search positioning statement answers: **what is this site the go-to source for, for whom, and why it (genuinely) over alternatives?**

- Derive it from the **real content** and the **user's input** on genuine differentiators. Ask directly: what do you actually do better/differently — real expertise, real focus, real proof?
- A useful shape: *"For [audience], [site] is the [category] that [genuine differentiator], because [real reason/proof]."*
- If the user can't name a real differentiator, **that is the finding** — surface it ("the site currently reads as undifferentiated; here's what that costs in search and AI citation") rather than inventing a claim. Undifferentiated, me-too content is exactly what struggles to rank or be cited.
- Positioning must show up **consistently** across the key pages and entity signals (name, schema `@id`, `sameAs`) — ties to the Cite rung. Consistency builds the entity engines trust.

> Never manufacture a market position. Spin and invented superlatives ("the leading…", "the #1…") with nothing real behind them are both untrue and ineffective.

---

## Topical authority — what to own

Engines (and AI answer engines) reward sites that demonstrably, comprehensively cover a topic — not sites that touch many unrelated things shallowly. So pick the topics you can genuinely own and go deep.

### Map it as pillars and clusters
- **Pillar (hub) topics** — the few core areas the site should be authoritative on, anchored to what it genuinely does and what the audience searches for.
- **Clusters** — the supporting sub-topics under each pillar (the questions, comparisons, how-tos, and details that make the coverage comprehensive).
- Map each pillar/cluster node to: an **existing page** (→ keep/improve/consolidate, via content-audit) or a **gap** (→ a create-brief for a human). The result is both a linking plan (Connect's hub-and-spoke) and a content backlog.

### Size and prioritise (with data if connected)
- **With a keyword tool (DataForSEO/Ahrefs):** real search volume and sub-topic demand size each opportunity; SERP/competitor data shows who owns it and whether you can realistically compete.
- **With Search Console:** topics where you already get impressions are footholds to build on; near-ranking queries are quick topical wins.
- **Without live data:** map topics from the domain and the user's expertise, and **label them un-sized** — a qualitative map is still directional, but don't present it as demand-validated.
- Prioritise: footholds you can extend + genuine, winnable gaps + high-relevance topics, balanced against effort.

---

## Competitive & gap analysis (honest)

- **With data:** identify who ranks for your target topics and the shape of winning content (depth, format, angle) — to find realistic gaps and a differentiated angle. Cite the data.
- **Without data:** a qualitative read from known competitors and the SERP, clearly labelled as inference — useful for direction, not a substitute for real numbers.
- **Gaps** become create-briefs (content-audit's "create" action): the topic, the intent, the questions to answer, the angle that fits the positioning. Briefs for humans — not auto-generated articles.
- Never fabricate competitor metrics or market claims. Inference labelled as inference; data cited as data.

---

## The deliverable
A short strategy the user can act on and hand to `seo-proposal-roadmap`:
1. **Positioning statement** (real, user-validated).
2. **Topical-authority map** — pillars → clusters → existing pages (action) / gaps (briefs).
3. **Priorities** — what to build first and why (demand/foothold/effort), with the basis labelled (data vs inference).
4. **Consistency notes** — how the positioning/differentiator should show up across pages and entity signals.

State the basis throughout and keep the boundary honest: a strong plan improves focus and odds; it doesn't guarantee you'll win a topic, and ongoing performance is live data to monitor.
